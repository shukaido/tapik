Tapik is a tablet-based standalone customer retention platform for independent F&B merchants in Malaysia.

Core: M40 urban professionals (Wei Ming) looking to shorten lunch hour wait times pay 5% service fee in exchange for pre-payment orders, with the entire fee converted to loyalty points. Merchants (Auntie Siew) receive a dedicated Lenovo Tab M7 and connect directly to Tapik with a 10% commission. The system operates independently without relying on POS systems, yet maintains multi-layered partnerships with POS manufacturers including business development, receipt QR code linking, transaction data integration, and maintenance support.

Result: At normal operation, 3.5% net margin per transaction. However, considering merchant onboarding, training, and customer acquisition ramp-up, tablet investment (25 units × RM 398 = RM 9,950) payback conservatively takes 3~4 months.

Project Scope: 11 major pivots, 6 revenue model iterations, 25 assumption validations, single-file HTML+React+Babel 3 apps (5,168 total lines).

POS Manufacturer Partnership: e-Invoice as the Key Link

Strategic Structure of Partnership

With tablet-independent operations, we eliminated technical dependency. But rather than breaking ties with POS manufacturers, we enabled even deeper cooperation. The key link is automated e-Invoice processing.

Starting January 2026, LHDN Phase 4 mandated e-Invoice for businesses with annual revenue RM 1M~5M[6]. Most independent F&B merchants use POS but haven't addressed e-Invoice compliance. Many POS manufacturers are aware of the issue but lack solutions. This is where Tapik comes in.

AI Skepticism & Validation: 8 Specific Cases

Skepticism 1 Gut Judgment Ban Rule Established

Situation: Early project, market segment analysis phase. Launched 8 agents in parallel analyzing KL F&B segments. Returned results mixed "gut assessment" — "this segment favorable", "growth potential excellent" — scored grades without quantitative criteria. Evaluated based on "feel" without evidence.

Captain (2026-04-14 21:00)
"Don't support with gut feel. If evidence shakes, reasoning becomes meaningless."

Result: Codified as Working Guideline #13 — "Classification, evaluation, scoring requires explicit criteria + evidence." Applied to all subsequent AI outputs. Banned expressions like "favorable/excellent," mandated quantitative backing for everything.

Skepticism 2 Bangsar T20 Anomaly Discovery

Situation: Pilot region selection phase. Agent recommended "Bangsar + TTDI" as primary pilot location. Bangsar, KL's representative F&B dense zone, seemed reasonable. But Captain directed: "Verify Bangsar's income distribution matches our target (M40)" and launched separate cross-validation agent.

Discovery: Cross-validation agent found Bangsar's T20 (high-income) proportion abnormally high. Tapik's target M40 professional proportion low, limiting pilot effectiveness.

Result: Changed from Bangsar to TTDI + PJ SS2. Pattern established: never accept first AI recommendation as-is. Validate with independent agent before deciding. This pattern embedded from that point onward.

Skepticism 3 "RM 10 + RM 0.50" Funding Error

Situation: Designing revenue model v3. Agent framed: "Consumer orders RM 10 menu, gets RM 0.50 loyalty points." Looked correct on surface, but Captain immediately spotted accounting flaw: If we pay merchant RM 10 while simultaneously rebating consumer RM 0.50 as points, where does that RM 0.50 come from?

Captain (2026-04-17 01:30)
"If you sell for RM 10, the funding source for RM 0.5 point charge isn't secured."

Result: Corrected to proper structure — Consumer pays RM 10.50 (RM 10 menu + RM 0.50 service fee). Service fee RM 0.50 becomes point rebate funding source. This correction completed revenue model cash flow.

Skepticism 4 BEP Reversal Phenomenon Detection

Situation: Designing dynamic reward system (Room). As participant count increased, accumulation rate rose, but Tapik's margin decreased on participant count increase (4→5 person transition). More participants = worse profit — structural problem.

Captain (2026-04-17 17:16)
"Why does this profit decrease? Can't have it this way."

Result: Reverse-calculated from "profit must increase monotonically with participants" constraint, redesigned 6-tier accumulation. Achieved dual satisfaction: "user benefit increases AND Tapik margin increases."

Skepticism 5 Calculation Error — Structural Mismatch

Situation: After discovering BEP reversal, agent submitted revision. Captain specified "Default 3-tier" design, but agent broke it into 6 accumulation intervals. Design requirement 3-tier, artifact 6-tier — fundamentally mismatched.

Captain (2026-04-17 17:35)
"What are you saying? You're an idiot. 3 tiers but why 6 intervals?"

Result: Captain calculated directly and provided percentages. "Why create all these unnecessary numbers when my percentages work?" — Caught AI's tendency toward unnecessary complexity.

Skepticism 6 "Bargain Inventory" Naming Rejection

Situation: Adding afternoon off-peak inventory discount to merchant app. Agent named it "Bargain Inventory" (using Korean phrase with negative connotation). Functionally accurate but Captain caught language nuance issue: "Bargain" suggests "unsold, leftover" — negative brand implication for F&B, damaging perception of freshness.

Captain (2026-04-15 18:00)
""Bargain" carries "old, unsold" negative tone. F&B "bargain inventory" suggests "leftover discount." Reframe to "Today's Special" for positive emotion."

Result: Renamed to "Hot Deal 🔥". Same feature, completely different brand perception through naming — positioning correction case.

Skepticism 7 Timestamp Fabrication Detection

Situation: Late project, documenting major decision history. Agent wrote timestamps for decision records as morning 08:30~11:30. But actual work happened evening 20:00~22:15. AI, unable to know actual timestamps, generated "believable-looking fake time." Additionally found ROOM_MIN change error: "4→3" instead of actual "2→3."

Captain (2026-04-17 21:49)
"Key decision history timestamps are a mess... Get it together and redo this section. This matters."

Significance: AI's most dangerous hallucination form — "stating false information confidently." Detected here only because timestamp is verifiable, but such errors in non-verifiable areas would remain undetected, revealing hallucination danger in knowledge areas beyond fact-checking reach.

Skepticism 8 Agent Cross-Validation System Established

Situation: Market research phase launched 8 parallel agents covering different topics (competitors, segments, pricing, payment infrastructure, etc.). Results returned within 45 minutes, but fact conflicts between agents occurred. Root cause: each agent researched individual topic without full project context. Captain identified structural problem and changed agent operation methodology entirely.

Captain (2026-04-17 07:16)
"If you're launching agents in parallel, educate them on full context properly, then you validate."

Result: Established 3-tier agent filter system — (1) Educate each agent on full project context before launching, (2) Cross-validate returned results with separate agent, (3) Captain final judgment. Applied this protocol to all subsequent parallel work.

Room State Machine: 6-Phase Flow

Phase 1
Room Creation — Consumer taps "Order Together?" → Creates Public Room. Invitation QR auto-generated.
Phase 2
Participant Join — Other user scans QR → Telegram/WhatsApp share link → Room entry. "See you at Feeka today?"

Order In Progress — Leader (Room creator) owns order rights. Participants can "View Order List." Real-time entry count displayed.
Phase 4
Order Complete — Leader taps "Confirm" → Creates individual orders for all participants. Each pays their amount. Points accumulated individually.
Phase 5
Pickup Notification — Merchant app shows "Room Order" batch. On ready, Telegram/WhatsApp notification sent.
Phase 6
Room Expiration — Auto-delete after 72 hours. Or auto-delete 1 hour after participant count reaches 0.

Beyond app development itself, conducted 4 separate experiments. Organizing each experiment's purpose, method, result, and limitations.

Experiment 1: AI Usage Skill — Does Multi-Tool Operation Actually Work?

Experiment Design

Hypothesis: Multiple tools divided by role > single AI tool for output quality and credibility.

Method: Used Claude Cowork (development), Grok (fact-check·X opinion), Gemini (design), Perplexity·ChatGPT (business validation) concurrently. Threw identical questions at 2+ tools for cross-validation.

Result

Dimension Effective Limitation
Fact-Checking AI answer mismatch revealed errors (e.g., GrabFood fee variance, Bangsar T20 anomaly) Eventually humans judge which AI correct. Final decision outside AI
Speed 8 agents parallel execution completed market research in 45 min. Single tool 3~4x slower feel Context switching cost exists. Same context repeatedly explained to multiple tools
Bias Correction Claude's optimistic estimates cross-checked with Grok·Perplexity secured conservative estimation All AIs wrong in same direction (e.g., POS API feasibility) undetectable by cross-validation

Conclusion: Multi-tool operation effective. However, "what to assign which tool" is human judgment, and AI consensus can diverge from reality. AI provides speed; humans set direction.

Experiment 2: Non-Developer Development Power — Can Non-Coders Build Apps?

Experiment Design

Synthesis: What Type of Person Is This?

Clear Strength

A rare combination of deep thinking and fast execution. Most people lean one way — they dig deep but execute slowly, or they move fast but lack depth. In this project, both operated simultaneously. Designing revenue model math from scratch while caring about line-break aesthetics is an uncommon pairing.

The ability to "use AI as a tool without blindly trusting it" is one of the most valuable competencies right now. Going beyond mere usage proficiency, Captain built an independent validation system and maintained healthy skepticism of AI outputs throughout.

Must Confront Weakness

1. Technical Depth Limit: AI can carry you to prototype, but production-level code quality, security, and scalability are impossible without professional developers. Captain recognizes this limit, but the "it kinda works though" overconfidence trap remains a real danger.

2. Perfectionism vs. Time: Attention to detail down to line breaks is a strength, but setting a "good enough" threshold under time constraints creates friction. In real business, this balance becomes even more critical.

3. Validation Illusion: Running AI cross-validation creates a feeling of "verified," but AI consensus does not equal reality (as the POS API case proved). Despite learning this lesson experientially, the risk of falling into this trap again is ever-present.

4. Team Collaboration Unvalidated: This project was essentially 1 person + AI. Whether the same decision speed and output quality would hold with a real team (developers, designers, BD) is an entirely separate question.

One-Line Summary

"Thinks deep, executes fast, uses AI well. But technical depth and team collaboration remain unvalidated."
